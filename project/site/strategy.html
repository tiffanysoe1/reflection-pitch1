05.2 — STRATEGY

Distribution Strategy

Distribution functions as a flywheel: once a major client signs on, it creates momentum that draws others in. Every country is racing to establish its AI strategy, and no one wants to be left behind. The power of cold outreach shouldn't be underestimated. During my internship at the AISC lab - a role I landed through cold calling - I learned that the government moved fast - closing a HK$3B datacenter investment deal in under a week, a direct response to the Nvidia ban. In sales, I've always believed that chasing perfection against the competition is a trap. The real game is framing. Consider the economics of a simple newspaper transaction: the buyer pays $1 because the value the information more than the dollar; the seller accepts $1 because they value it more than the paper. Value is entirely subjective - and that subjectivity is the opening. If someone pays you $100,000, the figure isn't your objective worth. It is what they believe they will get in return. Master the framing, and you will improve the number.

When I was interning in the AISC lab, a job I got from cold-calling, the deal to invest $HK 3B in datacenters was done in a week.

For sales, one can never be perfect vs the competition. The strategy is about framing the solution you deliver. If you are paid $100,000 for a job, is that your actual value? Consider a simple transaction: If someone buys a newspaper for $1, they do so because they value the information more than $1. Conversely, the company sells it because they value $1 more than the newspaper. Value is inherently subjective - framing it correctly matters.

Model Capability

One big risk is the failure to deliver a superior product.

Reflection recently signed a deal with the Pentagon and led a $2.5B funding round, signalling a strong interest in its prototype.

Being selected alongside Google, Microsoft and OpenAI for the most sensitive classified networks (IL6 and IL7) implies that DoD has evaluated Reflection's technology and found it credible enough to trust, even in its pre-released state.

The risk is reduced, but not eliminated. Funding and government contracts buy time and credibility, but the company still needs to ship a competitive model to the broader market to fulfil the promise of becoming "DeepSeek of the West"

The "Open" Strategy Risk

Reflection plans to open its model weights for researchers to developers to use freely, but keeps training data and the full training pipeline propietary - planning to generate revenue from large enterprises and governments buildouts.

Once you release open-weights, you lose control. Any well resourced competitor can fine-tune, distill, or improve on those weights and offer a better/cheaper version. Meta absorbs this loss, but Reflection has no cushion. The more succesful the model, the more it gets commoditized.

I think marketing & branding will matter alot, in order to counterbalance the risk.

Winning Hearts, not just Minds.

In Romance of Three Kingdoms, Zhu Ge Liang, a brilliant military strategist, chose to serve LiuBei over the more powerful CaoCao. LiuBei showed up himself, three times, in person. It wasn't the promise of resources that won Zhu Ge Liang - it was sincerity, and the rare chance to be the deciding mind behind an underdog worth believing in.

From my previous experience of serving hedge fund managers with 20 years+ of experience, I've always felt that enterprise sales is a nuanced, intuitive process. There is always a temptation to lead purely with logic and reasoning. The Liu Bei story is a good reminder that human nature hasn't changed - that winning hearts matters as much as winning minds.

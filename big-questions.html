The big questions

The future and current state of AI Labs

Q1

The Reflection Future

According to Alex, serious enterprises will want their homegrown, user-data-tuned AI to run on-prem or in a leased AI factory. Clouds who fulfill all the compliance won't be enough. The art & science of LLM development would spread, and there will be a need for the service that Reflection and other open models bring. Reflection is trying to build the whole stack end-to-end, supplying the whole system enterprise[1]. In the era of a changing world order, relying on a third-party closed model provider creates significant strategic risk. The threat of a platform being shut off makes open models infrastructure a necessity rather than an option. 

What I'm curious about: If it took years for a F500 company to move from on-prem to cloud, how long will it take for existing F500 who are already API-ed to Anthropic to shift to a new on-prem platform, and how much are they incentivised to do so?  Claude suggested the workload shift will take 6-18 months.The security argument make sense, but given the "work" and human nature, will it end up being a hybrid model where high security workloads are routed to on-prem?

Q2

The Anthropic Future

In a recent interview*, Dario mentioned that every community that AI has gone through has a set of heuristics about how things work - "commodization, moats, which layer has the value".  He believed that performance and cost of running inference matter far more than the visibility of the weight. The open-weight framing, he suggested, is a red herring. The assumption that more contributors means better outcomes - that progress is somehow additive - doesn't hold in this domain the way it does in traditional open-source software. When Dario thinks of competition, he strips it down to two questions: Is it a good model? How much does it cost to run?

Q3

The Mistral Game 

I've watched a couple of interviews that Misha(Reflection) and Timothee/Arthur (Mistral) made. It maps onto a deeper strategic question: where does the value in AI actually accrue?

Misha argues that intelligence is the product. Reflection's wager is that closing the gap between open-weights and frontier labs is the highest ROI investment a lab can make.

Mistral's position seemed to be more conservative. Their bet is on the stack: building pipelines, tooling and software infrastructure that make the model genuinely useful in deployment. The insight here is that enterprise buyers don't purchase intelligence in the abstract - they purchase reliable systems. The EU AI Act has become its structural moat, where data residency, auditability and regulatory alignment are prerequisites.

The verdict: Mistral's "usefulness over intelligence" framing only holds if the model quality is good enough to begin with. If a competitor releases a model that is both more capable and equally deployable, the stack advantage collapses. Mistral's bet is therefore implicitly a bet that model quality above a threshold stops being the most important variable for its target customers.

MY QUESTION: Is Mistral's head start on building enterprise relationships and EU regulatory credibility going to affect Reflection? My intuitive take is that both companies are fishing in different ponds. If Reflection delivers a far better model, it will put pressure on Mistral's Non-EU revenue.
Q4

The Current DeepSeek Game

Deepseek is strategically priced to be extremely competitive. In March 2025, they announced a theoretical cost-profit margin of their reasoning system at 545%. However, actual revenue is lower due to heavy discounts and a large portion of users accessing the service for free.

The information given was: $2/hour of GPU leasing per day, $87,072 GPU cost per day and $562,027 revenue per day.

This leads to my estimates of: 1,814 inference GPUs that run 24/7. Assuming a 78% input token at $0.55 and 22% output token at $2.19, revenue is $0.911 per 1M tokens. This means they process 617 billion tokens per day. As of May 7, 2026, negotiations indicate that DeepSeek's valuation is currently estimated at $515 billion USD. The $7.35B Series A includes $3B of Liang's own contribution. He is committed to achieving AGI.

Q5

Other Chinese Models

Baichuan AI has decided to focus on medical vertical large models, giving up on pre-training new foundational LLMs. The release of DeepSeek's open-weights high-performance models made it difficult to justify other startups' immense cost of training their own foundational model.
